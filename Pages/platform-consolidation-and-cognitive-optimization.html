Platform Consolidation and Cognitive Optimization

The Foundation: Why Consolidation Matters 

Before we dive into analytics, we need to revisit a concept from Module 1: platform fragmentation.

Remember the "digital scavenger hunt" problem? Students forced to toggle between multiple platforms, remember different passwords, and navigate inconsistent interfaces? That fragmentation doesn't just annoy students; it creates measurable cognitive costs that show up in your analytics data.

Let's explore why consolidation is the prerequisite for everything else.

The Hidden Cost of Platform Fragmentation

When you ask students to use multiple platforms in one course, such as Canvas for assignments, YouTube for videos, Google Docs for collaboration, Padlet for discussions, and Kahoot for quizzes, you're asking them to constantly switch between contexts.

Each switch has a cost.

What Are Mental Switching Costs?

Mental switching cost refers to the cognitive effort required to shift attention, adapt to new interface rules, and recall where you left off when transitioning between platforms (Tcherdakoff et al., 2025).

This is about: 

  • Remembering different login credentials
  • Reorienting to different navigation structures
  • Adapting to different visual layouts
  • Holding multiple mental models in working memory simultaneously

For neurotypical students, this is annoying. For neurodivergent students, it's exhausting, and it's reflected in the data.

The Analytics Evidence

When students navigate fragmented systems, you see:

  • Engagement drops after transitions between platforms
  • Higher abandonment rates when assignments require multiple tools
  • Increased time-to-completion as students waste cognitive energy on navigation
  • Login pattern gaps where students get lost in the shuffle

These aren't motivation issues. They're cognitive load issues.

The Solution: Consolidation

Consolidating course content into a single Learning Management System (LMS) eliminates mental switching costs (Le Cunff et al., 2024).

When everything lives in one place:

  • Students develop automated navigation patterns
  • Working memory is freed up for actual learning
  • Predictable structure reduces anxiety and cognitive load
  • You can actually track engagement accurately (no data scattered across platforms)

This is about being intentional. Every external tool you add increases cognitive load. Each one must justify its existence. Consolidation is the foundation. Now, let's talk about what happens when students struggle even within a consolidated system.
