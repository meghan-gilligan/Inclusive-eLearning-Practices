Land Acknowledgement

As we explore Inclusive eLearning Practices, we want to acknowledge that the tools and technologies we will be discussing today are not equally accessible to everyone.

Many Indigenous communities across Canada experience the digital divide, facing restricted access to the technology and infrastructure necessary for online learning. This is a result of colonialism, displacement, and systemic inequities.

As educators, we have a responsibility to thoughtfully and ethically engage with digital tools in a way that promotes inclusivity and equity for all.

We recognize that our ability to learn, research, and present on these topics is a privilege, made possible through structures built on stolen land and labour.

Each of the course developers lives, works, and enjoys the lands as settler allies. We recognize these lands belong to, and have been cared for by, the people of the Huron-Wendat Nation, Alderville First Nation, and Mississaugas of Scugog Island First Nation, branches of the greater Anishinaabeg Nation, including Algonquin, Ojibway, Odawa, and Pottawatomi. These lands remain home to numerous Indigenous nations and peoples.

To know what land you reside on, please visit https://www.whose.land/en/.
