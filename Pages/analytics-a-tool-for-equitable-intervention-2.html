Analytics: A Tool for Equitable Intervention

Analytics allow you to move beyond gut feelings and surface-level impressions to determine if your course truly supports all learners (Le Cunff et al., 2024). But not all analytics are created equal. You need three types working together:

1. Engagement Analytics: Who's Struggling to Navigate?

Purpose: Measures student interaction with the platform (e.g., login frequency, time spent on course pages).

What It Reveals: Successful consolidation should show sustained engagement with consistent login patterns. Significant gaps or erratic patterns signal struggles with navigation or cognitive overload (Le Cunff et al., 2024).

Example: A student logs in daily for two weeks, then disappears for five days, then returns sporadically. This isn't laziness; it's a red flag that something in your course structure is creating barriers.

Action: When you spot irregular engagement, reach out with a low-pressure check-in: "I noticed you haven't logged in recently. Is everything okay? Is there anything about the course structure that's confusing?"

2. Performance Analytics: Who's Facing Executive Function Challenges?

Purpose: Measures academic outcomes, assignment submission rates, completion timing, and assessment scores.

What It Reveals: Neurodivergent students may be disproportionately missing deadlines or struggling with time-sensitive assessments, indicating challenges with executive function in task initiation and time management (Woodbury et al., 2024).

Example: A student consistently submits assignments 2-3 days late, even with reminders. This isn't procrastination; it's difficulty with temporal sequencing and task initiation.

Action: Offer proactive adjustments aligned with UDL, such as flexible deadlines, alternative assessment formats, or structured checklists that break tasks into smaller steps (Frolli et al., 2023).

3. Predictive Analytics: Who Needs Intervention NOW?

Purpose: Combines engagement + performance indicators to identify at-risk students before challenges escalate (Liz-Domínguez et al., 2019).

What It Reveals: Subtle patterns that traditional grading systems miss, like sporadic logins combined with late submissions, that indicate a student is struggling to manage the course demands.

Example: A student has medium engagement (logging in but not completing tasks) + declining performance (missing deadlines) = medium risk flag.

Action: This is your cue for early intervention. Reach out, offer support, and adjust course elements if needed.

The Ethics of Analytics

Using analytics for accessibility is powerful, but it requires ethical responsibility:
Privacy: Use data to support, never to shame or surveil
Equity: Don't assume struggles indicate a lack of ability
Action: Don't just collect data, actually intervene when students need help
Transparency: Let students know how you're using analytics to support them

Analytics transform accessibility from a theoretical commitment into a measurable and accountable practice. You're not guessing who needs help; you're using evidence to provide it.
