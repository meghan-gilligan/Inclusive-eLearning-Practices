Why Inclusive eLearning Matters

Understanding The Challenge

Neurodivergent students often face a fractured learning environment with multiple systems and platforms to navigate. This can lead to:

  • Cognitive overload from juggling too many different tools and interfaces
  • Navigation difficulties when trying to find course materials and resources
  • Reduced focus on actual learning content

The problem gets worse when courses lack consistent design patterns (Le Cunff et al., 2024). When each course looks and works differently, students spend valuable energy just figuring out how to access information, leaving less mental capacity for the learning itself.

The Cost of Exclusion: Barriers and Burdens

When e-learning environments are designed for neurotypical learners by default, they inadvertently create significant, measurable barriers for neurodivergent (ND) learners.

Inclusive eLearning

Inclusive eLearning aligns education with modern global goals, shifting the perspective from a limiting medical model to one that emphasizes identity and culture.

Global Equity: Directly supports the UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), specifically Goal 4: Quality Education and Goal 10: Reduced Inequalities, ensuring access for vulnerable groups, including persons with disabilities (Brito & Dias, 2020).

Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion (DEI): Moves beyond simple "accommodation" towards recognizing neurodiversity as a culture and identity. This approach actively combats stigma and promotes the social and political inclusion of all learners (Dwyer et al., 2023).

The Learner's Invisible Burden

Neurodivergent students are often forced to perform "invisible access work," defined as the cognitive, emotional, and administrative effort undertaken by students beyond their formal academic tasks (Tcherdakoff et al., 2025). This includes troubleshooting poor navigation, navigating multiple platforms or digital tools, and manually reformatting inaccessible files, while constantly self-advocating.

This labour is required because higher ed digital systems, Learning Management Systems (LMSs), and educational content are frequently designed with neurotypical users as the default, inadvertently creating or failing to remove systemic barriers for ND students.

Result: Energy that should be spent on learning is instead drained by accessing content, undermining academic performance and emotional health (Le Cunff et al., 2024; Tcherdakoff et al., 2025).
