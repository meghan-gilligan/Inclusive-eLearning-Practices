Understanding Neurodiversity

Before we dive into design principles, we want to establish a common language and understanding. The foundation of inclusive eLearning is recognizing and respecting the natural variation in how people learn. Having clear terms helps us focus on designing environments that support everyone.

Key Definitions

Neurodiversity is a concept and perspective that suggests human brains naturally vary. It is the idea that differences in brain function, such as those related to attention, learning, and mood, are simply part of the natural variation of the human population, similar to differences in ethnicity or sexual orientation. This term promotes the acceptance of different brain wiring as a normal and valuable form of human diversity (Singer, 2021).

Neurodivergence refers to having a brain that functions in ways that significantly differ from the majority. An individual whose brain functions this way is described as neurodivergent. It encompasses anyone whose brain works differently, whether from birth (such as autism, ADHD, dyslexia, dyspraxia, dyscalculia) or through life experiences (such as traumatic brain injury, PTSD, epilepsy, or mental health conditions). The challenges faced by neurodivergent individuals often arise when environments are designed primarily for the "neurotypical" majority (Singer, 2021).

​Common Neurodivergences

  • Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) is a neurodevelopmental condition that presents ongoing problems with attention, hyperactivity, and impulsiveness. Symptoms include difficulty focusing, forgetfulness, restlessness, and trouble staying organized (HealthLink BC, 2022).
  • Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) is a developmental difference affecting how people communicate, interact socially, and behave. People with ASD may communicate, learn, or act in ways that are different from others, and they often have particular strengths as well as challenges. ASD is called a “spectrum” because there is a wide range of abilities and behaviours among autistic people (National Institute of Mental Health, 2023).
  • Dyslexia is a condition that makes it difficult to read, spell, and sometimes write, even with proper instruction. It is caused by differences in how the brain processes written language (Dyslexia Canada, 2023).​
  • Dysgraphia is a condition that makes writing by hand difficult, including trouble with spelling, organizing ideas, and forming letters. People with dysgraphia struggle to write clearly and quickly (International Dyslexia Association, 2015).​
  • Dyscalculia is a condition that makes it hard to understand numbers and math. People with dyscalculia struggle to do calculations or learn math facts. This condition is also referred to as “math learning disability” (Learning Disabilities Association of America, 2023).​
  • Dyspraxia, also known as Developmental Coordination Disorder (DCD), is a condition that makes it difficult to coordinate physical movements. It can cause clumsiness or difficulty with tasks such as writing, using tools, or playing sports (NHS, 2024).

Optional Additional Resources

  • Neurodiverse Sensitive Language Guide: This guide, developed by La Trobe University, outlines respectful language and symbols for discussing neurodiversity in a culturally sensitive manner. It provides recommendations such as using identity-first terms (for example, “Autistic person”), avoiding outdated labels like “high functioning”, and choosing appropriate symbols that reflect neurodiversity.
  • Neurodiversity Session 3: Universal Design for Learning & Neurodiversity in Higher Education: This video is a presentation from The Center for Inclusive Excellence about applying the Universal Design for Learning (UDL) framework to support neurodiversity in higher education. It explains the three core UDL principles of engagement, representation, and action/expression, and introduces the "plus one" strategy as a practical way to implement these inclusive practices.
  • Understanding the ADHD Brain: This interactive H5P module explains how ADHD affects brain function, specifically the role of neurotransmitters like dopamine and norepinephrine. It also explores how different types of medications work to regulate brain activity and manage symptoms, as well as their common side effects.
  • Autism Scenario - Max: This accordion-style H5P element presents a scenario about a student named Max facing significant distress and academic barriers. You can click on different headings to  read through the parts of the scenario and gain insight into the specific factors contributing to his distress and consider appropriate strategies to create a more inclusive and supportive learning environment for him.
