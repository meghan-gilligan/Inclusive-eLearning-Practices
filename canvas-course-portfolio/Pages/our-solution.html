Our Solution

Student-Centred UDL Implementation

We've examined the problem in depth, including fragmented systems, cognitive overload, and invisible burden. So what's the solution? It starts with proactive design and centring student voices.

Research with neurodivergent students shows that technology can create more comfortable and engaging learning experiences (Durgungoz & Durgungoz, 2025). Key findings include:

  • Anonymous participation tools like Padlet help students engage without social pressure
  • Creative digital platforms such as Canva allow for authentic self-expression
  • Balanced interaction that avoids both passive lectures and overwhelming group discussions

Neurodivergent students have told us they want to participate actively, but traditional group discussions can feel overwhelming. Digital tools offer a middle ground where students can contribute meaningfully while maintaining control over their level of interaction.

This approach prioritizes co-design with neurodivergent students themselves rather than relying solely on expert opinions or theoretical frameworks. By listening to student voices, we can create learning environments that truly meet their needs.

The Power of UDL: A Proven Framework

The solution employs a proactive design approach, utilizing the Universal Design for Learning (UDL) framework, which has been empirically validated to enhance engagement and outcomes for all learners (Durgungoz & Durgungoz, 2025).

UDL's Three Principles

UDL provides a blueprint for creating flexible learning environments by addressing three core brain networks (CAST, 2024):

Table 1: UDL principles and examples of inclusive strategies that address diverse ways of perceiving, expressing, and engaging with learning content.
Principle What it Addresses Example of Implementation
Representation: The "What" How we gather facts and categorize information. Provide information in multiple formats. Use text, audio, video, and accurate transcripts.
Action & Expression: The "How" How we organize and express ideas. Allow alternative assessment methods, such as written papers, video presentations, or infographics.
Engagement: The "Why" How learners are motivated and sustained. Offer choice and control over tasks; use anonymous interaction, such as polls, instead of mandatory speaking.

We'll explore each of these UDL principles in much greater depth in Module 2, including concrete before-and-after examples you can apply immediately.

The Importance of Learner Voice

Effective inclusive design adheres to the disability advocacy slogan: "Nothing about us without us" (Dwyer et al., 2023; Tcherdakoff et al., 2025).

Designs co-developed with neurodivergent individuals are significantly more effective because they address real-world pain points, such as the preference for anonymous, technology-mediated participation over overwhelming group discussions (Durgungoz & Durgungoz, 2025).

Shared Responsibility: System vs. Content

Achieving accessibility is a shared ethical and pedagogical duty, requiring attention at both the platform level and the instructional level.

Table 2: Platform and instructor responsibilities in ensuring accessibility, usability, and cognitive load reduction in digital learning environments.
The Platform's Role The Instructor’s Role
AODA & WCAG Compliance: Technical standards for navigation, keyboard use, alternative text, screen reader compatibility (Ghislandi, 2012; University of Maryland Global Campus, n.d.). Clarity & Simplicity: Tidy content page, ample white space, one core message per visual.
Seamless Integration: Unified platforms to reduce login friction and cognitive load. Cognitive Load Management: Break down complex tasks and figures; avoid reading long text; build in break reminders.
Assistive Technology Compatibility: Ensuring screen readers, magnifiers, and alternative input devices function correctly. Technical Standards: High-contrast, large, sans-serif fonts; accurate, high-quality captions.

Inclusive eLearning matters because it transforms education from a system that tolerates difference into one that proactively celebrates and leverages neurodiversity. By moving beyond compliance to adopt a UDL framework, institutions and instructors can eliminate the "coping tax" students currently pay, leading directly to increased engagement, higher retention rates, and better academic outcomes for the entire student body.
