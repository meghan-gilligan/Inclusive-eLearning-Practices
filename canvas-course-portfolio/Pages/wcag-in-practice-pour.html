WCAG Made Simple: The POUR Framework

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) sound intimidating, but they boil down to four simple principles you can remember with the acronym POUR:

Perceivable • Operable • Understandable • Robust

Let's break down what each one actually means:

Source: University of Minnesota Duluth IT Systems & Services (https://itss.d.umn.edu/articles/wcag-principles)

Perceivable: Can All Students Access the Information?

The Problem: If content only exists in one format, some students literally cannot perceive it.

  • A student who is blind can't see your infographic
  • A student who is deaf can't hear your video lecture
  • A student with colorblindness might miss colour-coded information

The Solution: Provide content in multiple perceivable formats

  • Add alt text to all images so screen readers can describe them
  • Include captions on videos so Deaf students can read what's said
  • Provide transcripts so content can be read AND heard
  • Never use colour alone to convey meaning (add icons, labels, or patterns too)

Real Example: Instead of posting just a video lecture, provide the video with captions, a full transcript, and the slide deck. Same content, multiple ways to perceive it. Sound familiar? That's UDL Principle #1 in action!

Operable: Can ALL Students Navigate and Use Your Course?

The Problem: If your course requires a mouse, students who use keyboards, voice commands, or other assistive technologies are locked out.

The Solution: Ensure all interactive elements work without a mouse

  • Make sure all links, buttons, and forms can be accessed with keyboard (Tab, Enter, Space keys)
  •  Provide clear focus indicators so users know where they are on the page
  • Don't set automatic time limits on activities
  • Make navigation consistent and predictable across all pages

Real Example: That clickable image carousel on your homepage? Ensure students can tab through images and activate them with the Enter key, not just by clicking with a mouse.

Understandable: Can ALL Students Comprehend Your Content?

The Problem: Complex navigation, unclear instructions, or inconsistent layouts create confusion, especially for students with cognitive disabilities, ADHD, or those who are learning in a second language.

The Solution: Make content clear, predictable, and easy to understand

  • Use plain language (aim for Grade 8-10 reading level)
  • Organize content with clear headings (H1, H2, H3 in proper hierarchy)
  • Keep navigation consistent across all course pages
  • Provide clear instructions with examples
  • Avoid jargon or define technical terms

Real Example: Instead of "Submit your artifact via the LMS interface by EOD," write "Upload your assignment to Canvas by 11:59 PM tonight."

Robust: Does Your Content Work Across ALL Technologies?

The Problem: If your content only works on certain devices or with certain software, you're excluding students who use different tools.

The Solution: Build content that works everywhere

  •  Avoid scanned PDFs—use searchable, screen-reader-friendly formats
  • Test content on mobile devices, not just computers
  •  Use standard HTML rather than proprietary formats
  •  Ensure compatibility with screen readers, magnifiers, and voice control

Real Example: That PDF you made by scanning a printed document? A screen reader can't read it. Convert it to a proper text-based PDF or Word document.
