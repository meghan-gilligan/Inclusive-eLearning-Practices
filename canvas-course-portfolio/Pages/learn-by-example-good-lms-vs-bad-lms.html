Learn by Example: Good LMS vs. Bad LMS

Accessible vs. Inaccessible: Spot the Difference

You know the POUR principles. Now let's see them in action, or in their absence.

Below, you'll see a side-by-side comparison of two LMS homepages: one that creates barriers and one that removes them. As you examine both examples, notice how small design choices have a significant impact on usability.

Ask yourself as you look: "Which course would I rather navigate if I had low vision? If I used a screen reader? If I struggled with executive function and needed clear visual organization?"

The differences are dramatic, and they're all fixable.

Visual Comparison: LMS Homepage Design

What Makes the Good LMS Accessible?

The good LMS example demonstrates several critical accessibility features:

  • High contrast (7:1 ratio) - Meets WCAG AAA standards, making text readable for users with low vision, older adults, and anyone viewing the screen in bright sunlight
  • Clear visual hierarchy - Important information stands out through size, colour, and placement
  • Organized navigation - Clean sidebar with icons and labels makes it easy to find what you need
  • Descriptive labels - Course names like "Mathematics 101 - Calculus I" instead of cryptic codes
  • Colour-coded urgency indicators - Red for urgent, orange for soon, green for on track (with icons too, not just colour)
  • Visual progress tracking - Students can see at a glance how they're doing in each course
  • Relevant, filtered information - Only announcements and updates that matter to the student
  • Consistent formatting - Dates, times, and information presented uniformly throughout

What Makes the Bad LMS Inaccessible?

The bad LMS example shows common accessibility failures that create barriers:

  • Low contrast text (2.5:1 ratio) - Fails WCAG standards, difficult to read for many users
  • Cluttered navigation - Over 20 menu items visible at once, overwhelming users
  • Cryptic course names - "COURSE_2024_FALL_MATH_101_SECTION_A_REVISED_V3" is impossible to parse quickly
  • Notification spam - Important course updates buried under irrelevant system messages
  • No visual hierarchy - Everything looks equally important, so nothing stands out
  • Information overload - Activity feed shows notifications from courses you're not even enrolled in
  • Inconsistent formatting - Dates shown as "2025-11-01 23:59:59 EST" and "Nov 5", and "TBD"
  • Technical jargon - Footer cluttered with session IDs and IP addresses students don't need
